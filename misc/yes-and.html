Yes, and …

Learning to write formulas in Excel was my first conscious brush with automation. I daydreamed my chores into the hands of machines. Need another transition here When I read Diamond Age, where a girl learns from a book, the Primer, that generates lessons for her based on her life experiences, I wanted it.

In 2017, a new architecture for neural networks was created, and tasks like summarizing a news article suddenly seemed with reach. It was called the Transformer, and I was starting a new job at research lab where it was invented1. It seemed like it would be possible to build the literal Primer in a few short years. Colleagues in my building rushed to create its predecessors, and I froze.

An important part of the Primer is that its lessons are tailored to the individual. Summarizing a news article requires knowing what the reader is hoping to come away from the article with. If they’re looking for the facts of the case, which facts were relevant? And also, what is a fact? A single summary could not give you the salient takeaways. Actually, it’s the opposite, communicating all interesting parts of an article might require expanding the article to bring in additional context. So while the Primer was maybe the inspiration, the actual products that were deployed set their goals much shorter than surfacing tailored information to the reader. The language tools that have come closest to this are: SmartSummaries that mine your emails for actionable takeaways2, or predictive coding[cite, microsoft / openai][There are many other AI-based language products, like AI Dungeon that are astounding and wildly transformative.].

At work and at weekend, SF parties, I waded through conversations: “Wouldn’t it be so cool if we could…,” “Oh! And then we could…” The conversations would spin out as the participants discussed the technical challenges of implementing the new task.

And I would respond with “sure, but.”

How much data would we have to collect about someone in order to tailor a summary to them? Couldn’t it be a violation of their privacy? Sure, but learning is awfully close to persuasion, and persuasion is a dangerous, powerful tool. Sure, but who gets to decide what’s important for people to learn? Shouldn’t that decision be made by the community?

How come “yes, and”s were allowed to have fun with their work? How come they weren’t thinking about the “but”s? I wanted to have fun with my work. Somehow in the decade between my first foray into programming and today being confronted with the opportunity to realize those dreams, I didn’t know if I wanted it anymore.

I learned this is another form of privilege–the ability to turn a blind eye to the effects your actions have on others without it meaningfully impacting your life.

I wasn’t the only “sure, but” in the room. They came in all forms: AI researchers arguing that these algorithms are no more than parrots that return whatever data they were used to train on3, philosophers formalizing how people make decisions about what level of privacy are expected in a given situation4, and researchers that worked to us to specify nuanced objectives to AI agents through offering feedback5, When I started to look, I was forced to confront my own ignorance. For decades, linguistics has had an ongoing debate about what models of language, like the Transformer, can reasonably be used for. Sociologists, historians, and librarians have been navigating the ethics and challenges of data collected from, or left behind by humans also for decades. Data availability and the process through which that data was collected would influence both the types of research that were possible and the systems that were built.

It’s perhaps inexcusable that AI researchers are mostly brought up without ready exposure to prior work in these other disciplines. It’s perhaps an excuse to say that our field had just undergone a revolution when so many researchers have had underlying goals of understanding or recreating human cognition since the beginnings of AI as a discipline. But somehow, the right people were not yet in touch, and through the work of many, the last few years have seen people across disciplines slowly meeting each other and translating their disciplines for each other.

And part of that is right. There are so many interesting questions to think about for each discipline. There must be a compelling reason to think about a specific question. Advances in technology makes particular questions more compelling: driverless cars revitalized the trolley problem, CRISPER ignited discussion about human evolution and meddling of science, and the previous age of AI saw discussion about [todo]. Society drives technology: the need for algorithmic decision making to perform just as well on marginalized groups as the majority group spurs research on algorithmic changes and changes to data collection procedures, the desire for machines to fail in similar ways to humans selects particular technical solutions over others, and People’s time and attention are limited and we re-prioritize as we gather more information.

Feel free to tell me that I’ve accepted the status quo too much. I certainty used to feel that the way things were was very wrong.

For sure, the technologies that are now available bring to front a different set of questions than the past decade. While all have been studied and deliberated on, we’ve re-prioritized.

So, I think I’ve mislead you by setting this up as a dichotomy. The “yes, and”s ought not to be pitted against the “sure, but”s. They do not describe two types of people. One is not morally superior to the other because they call out problems. “Yes, and” is not just for the technologist excited about their line of work. It’s an attitude that is essential to any dreamer.

In the example I’ve given of my work, technology and human desires go hand in hand. Human desires for automation or privacy influence the open questions and changes the way technology is designed. I’m heartened by the “sure, but” line of questioning because it introduces new technical challenges to be resolved. This is not a novel thought to any designer. But the actual practice of invoking both “yes, and” and “sure, but” is where I find myself caught in a loop.

It’s very easy to flit between the two with half baked thoughts leading into each other. But you need to give yourself enough pause to explore an idea fully regardless of if it’s a “yes, and” or “sure, but” thought.
