Yes, and …

When I learned you could write formulas in Excel, I was floored. I spent the next week calculating anything. My dreams grew from there. I wanted a virtual assistant that could plan my day. I wanted to talk to my computer and have it go click through Neopets to collect the daily omelet (it was 2006). When I read Diamond Age, where a girl learns from a book, the Primer, that generates lessons for her based on her life experiences, I wanted it. I wanted it for me, for my baby brother, for everyone in the world.

And I wanted to be a part of making it.

In 2017, a new architecture for neural networks was created, and tasks like summarizing a news article suddenly seemed with reach. It was called the Transformer, and I was starting a new job at research lab where it was invented1. It seemed like it would be possible to build the literal Primer in a few short years. Colleagues in my building rushed to create its predecessors, and I froze.

An important part of the Primer is that lessons are tailored to the individual. Summarizing a news article required knowing what the reader was hoping to come away from the article with. Was it facts of the case? Which facts of the case were relevant? A single summary could not possibly capture all information that a piece had. Actually, it’s the opposite, communicating all interesting parts of an article might require expanding the article to bring in additional context. So while the Primer was maybe the inspiration, the actual products that were deployed set their goals much shorter than surfacing tailored information to the reader. The language tools that have flown closest to this are: SmartSummaries that mine your emails for actionable takeaways2, or predictive coding3.4

At work and at weekend, SF parties, I waded through conversations: “Wouldn’t it be so cool if we could…,” “Yes, yes! And…,” Oh! And then we could…" And I would respond: sure, but how much data would we have to collect about someone in order to tailor a summary to them? Couldn’t it be a violation of their privacy? Sure, but learning is awfully close to persuasion, and persuasion is a dangerous, powerful tool. Sure, but who gets to decide what’s important for people to learn? Shouldn’t that decision be made by the community?

I spent two years saying “sure, but.” The ease at which people around me wove narratives that through occlusion, plausible multiple interpretations [language juggling? crafty language?] seemingly justified anything made me feel completely unmoored from morals. I wondered if truth was an illusion and harm could be thought away5. For a moment, I trained in that dark art.

As “yes, and”s swirled around me, and I felt like I was living in another dimension. How come they weren’t thinking about the “but”s? It was blindingly naive. They saw all the upsides to their work, and seemingly none of the downsides. I hadn’t yet learned that privilege was a fantastic word to describe this. They weren’t thinking about the downsides because they didn’t have to. Few people they were accountable to asked6, and there was so much trust in technology, that many were excited to have as much data about their lives tracked as possible7. They were having fun dreaming up new technologies, and I was running around after them with only harms swimming in my head.

It made me very resentful. How come they were allowed to have fun, and I have to be the cleanup crew? Some of these messes you can’t even clean up. You just have to prevent. Who wants to be the downer every day? Around this time, I was shocked to learn that a non-trivial number of folks around me also parallelized by fear of the dual use of their work. I was denying myself the right to enjoy my work and feeling better-than-thou for thinking about potential impacts. A non-trivial number of folks around me don’t know what to work on, and are not excited. An shocking number of people, one person, I know exudes excitement for the field. I don’t respond well to constant fear. I think very few of us do. In the short term, fear might kick your adrenaline up. After a while, it does a number on you and turns you numb.

I’m still trying to find joy in what I do.

I’m trying to look for safe ways of exploring new technologies

I’m trying to involve ways to

These days, I try to let brainstorming sessions go on unquestioned for a while, before cutting in with “okay now what are the sure, buts.” But that’s a correction for me, not a directive. If you’re doing a lot of “yes, anding” and no “sure, butting” then you need the opposite correction.

From the other thing

But just like conversations around privilege, it does nobody any good if you sit around feeling bad about how much privilege you have. And honestly, I flatter myself with the amount of impact I think I could have.

I don’t mean to say that researchers have overcorrected and are now too worried about the impact of AI on society. Like many other communities, ours has pockets that are insufficiently concerned in addition to pockets that have laboriously advocated for awareness of harms of AI and pockets, where I also reside, that are paralyzed with the fear of missteps. It feels wrong to have fun in a fraught field.
