Yes, and …

When I learned you could write formulas in Excel, I was floored. I spent the next week calculating anything. My dreams grew from there. I wanted a virtual assistant that could plan my day. I wanted to talk to my computer and have it go click through Neopets to collect the daily omelet (it was 2006). When I read Diamond Age, where a girl learns from a book, the Primer, that generates lessons for her based on her life experiences, I wanted it. I wanted it for me, for my new-born brother, for everyone in the world.

And I wanted to be a part of making it.

In 2017, a new architecture for neural networks was created, and tasks like summarizing a news article suddenly seemed with reach. It was called the Transformer, and I was starting a new job at research lab where it was invented1. It seemed like it would be possible to build the literal Primer in a few short years. Colleagues in my building rushed to create its predecessors, and I froze.

An important part of the Primer is that lessons are tailored to the individual. Summarizing a news article required knowing what the reader was hoping to come away from the article with. Was it facts of the case? Which facts of the case were relevant? A single summary could not possibly capture all information that a piece had. Actually, it’s the opposite, communicating all interesting parts of an article might require expanding the article to bring in additional context. So while the Primer was maybe the inspiration, the actual products that were deployed set their goals much shorter than surfacing tailored information to the reader. The language tools that have flown closest to this are: SmartSummaries that mine your emails for actionable takeaways2, or predictive coding[cite, microsoft / openai][There are many other AI-based language products, like AI Dungeon that are astounding and wildly transformative.].

At work and at weekend, SF parties, I waded through conversations: “Wouldn’t it be so cool if we could…,” “Oh! And then we could…” And I then I would respond with how I wasn’t sure how we could possibly create whatever the latest thing was while

I spent two years only seeing the potential dangers. Instead of “yes, and” I was saying “sure, but.” “I suppose you could do that, but have you considered….” Am I just trying to justify what is impossible to justify? Is it completely morally corrupt? For a moment there, I believed that you could make anything sound plausible. I felt completely unmoored from morals. The seeming ease with which people around me crafted narratives that for the first five minutes sounded exciting made me doubt the validity of everything.

And so come with me for a moment as we spiral down “sure, but.” Sure, but how much data would we have to collect about someone in order to tailor a summary to them? Couldn’t it be a violation of their privacy? Sure, but learning is awfully close to persuasion, and persuasion is a dangerous, powerful tool. Sure, but who gets to decide what’s important for people to learn? Shouldn’t that decision be made by the community?

Around me, “yes, and”s swirled, and I felt like I was living in another dimension. How come they were allowed to have fun with their work? How come they weren’t thinking about the “but”s. I wanted to have fun with my work. Somehow in the decade between my first foray into programming and being confronted with the opportunity to realize my dreams, I didn’t know if I wanted it anymore. I just hadn’t vocalized it to myself, or to others. If no one else was talking about it, was I just being needlessly cautious3? I guess this is what priveledge looks like. I didn’t realize how important “yes, and” was to brainstorming. I didn’t realize that not doing it was keeping me from having fun, from actually enjoying my work, and from being invested in it. “Sure, but”s are incredibly important, it’s just worth it to go a little

I wasn’t the only “sure, but” in the room as I soon found out.
