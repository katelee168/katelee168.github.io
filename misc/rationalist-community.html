The Rationalist Community circa 2016-2018 and its growth

August 2021

I was first exposed to the rationalist and effective altruist communities at Princeton in 2016. Moving to the bay area to work in AI research, I mingled with many folks in the scene. I wouldn’t call myself a member of the community, but my continual encounters has forced me to question why I find the community both so enticing and why I hold back so much from full immersion. Here’s a collection of thoughts.

Perfectionism

I, too, felt responsible for the parts of the world that I had control over. I thought, in my perfectionist way, that I had to do the best job ever in dealing with it. The community seemed to feel responsible for figuring out the best solution for the world’s problems. Doing a net-positive thing, somehow didn’t feel good enough. It had to solve the global optimization problem.

My perfectionist tendencies loved global optimization problems. I factored everyone in my life into my grand schemes, giving my friends complicated schedules planned down to the fifteen minute mark of where we would be and who we would be meeting up with. I planned no slack. If we got off schedule, we were behind.

Most times, it worked out. I could cram an impossible number of hangouts, games, and chores in a day. When it didn’t work out, I melted into a puddle of anxiety and stress. If someone else was late, or god forbid the train was late, I frantically rescheduled everything else that day.

I’ve been told my capacity for dealing with logistics is a great strength. I’m here to say that I’ve completely burned it out. Today, the thought of planning for anything is terrifying. If I have to transport my body between cities, I can get literally nothing else done that day.

So I don’t think always trying to solve the global optimization problem is a good thing. Even if you think you know what the solution is, it doesn’t fall to your shoulders to implement it. People have different strengths, and we can contribute to different capacities. Do something you enjoy doing that makes the neighborhood around you a little better. Everyone experiences and sees different things, you can be open to helping in ways that you enjoy and are good at, but just because you could do something, doesn’t mean you have to.

This certaintly ties into the tech scene’s focus on scale. Solving a problem for just the few people around you is not enough. You must at least have a plan for bringing it across the country if not to the whole world.

A failure mode:

I have a friend who decided several years ago that he loved another individual’s research and wanted to help advance it as much as possible. This friend signed up for a job working with the researcher and doing all the grunge infrastructure work. He hated it. Surely some people love setting up machines to run complicated models in parallel, but he was not one of them. He still felt that he wanted to contribute, and furthering this research felt like the most important thing he could be doing. He couldn’t shake the feeling that he was incompetent and useless. I’m sure he wasn’t. His body was just revolting when his mind wouldn’t let himself go.

My friend was medicated and self medicating, and I wished he could just close his eyes for a moment and let himself do something that made just him happy. Then, maybe he could find a thing that made him happy and did a small good in the world. It felt like he wasn’t allowed to put himself first.

To be honest, I wish I could do that too. But, easier said than done, it’s hard to say what brings me joy! And I find so many causes meaningful! There are many possible lives one could live.

Collective action

All that said, I’ve always respected how effectively the rationalist community could rally behind a cause. Since people bought the idea that they should do the most effective thing, all it took was determining a “thing.”

I’m simplifying, of course, but the number of people who make big life changes to advance the cause is impressive to me.

This might also be true with other movements as well, and perhaps with political campaigns. I just happen to have the front row seats to this one.

The frequent discussions about long-term AI risk in the rationalist community ran absolutely counter to my work place where up until very recently, short-term AI risk (like perpetuating harmful stereotypes in decision making systems and violating privacy expectations of data used for training) were not even widely discussed.

It feels trauma inducing

Being told that you can and must do the most good for the world is exhausting and also completely wrong. The world is made up of systems, and systems are made of individuals who have influence on the system but are not the system. Thinking in systems is certaintly a framework within the community and they would completely agree that finding the leverage points in the system and taking collective action is important. However, it feels like people take on a lot of individual responsibility
