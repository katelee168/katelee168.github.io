Why I started a PhD

I’m doing a PhD at Cornell and working at Google part-time. Before this, I worked at Google Brain as a researcher for four years. I’ve been asked a lot why I made this decision, so I thought I’d try to write out my thought process and where I’m at now, one year in to the PhD, both for you and for me.

I’d love to hear any thoughts you have on this. If you’re thinking of doing a PhD, I’m hope this is helpful!

You can write to me at my github handle at gmail dot com.

I wanted to.

Every year for three years leading up to going to my PhD, I wondered if that year would be the year I would apply for a PhD. Growing up in an area where many people had doctorates and scientific research was widely promoted, doing research and getting my PhD at some point was a default for me.

Eventually, I said, heck the only way I’ll stop thinking about if I should go do one is if I get up and go do one. This is, as it turns out, not super true. It’s possible to just decide that you don’t need to do one in this life. Several of us in the position of doing research in industry without a PhD got together to chat about whether a PhD would make sense. In that group, I’m the only one who went to do a PhD and it’s not obvious that there was a right answer here.

I was dissatisfied with my work.

It seems like the people who have left industry have often felt dissatisfied with their work in some way. Makes sense, it’s a huge activation energy to change jobs, let alone go back to school, and you might only consider that if you had good reason to believe you would like research much more than what you were currently doing–either because research was really great, or because your job really sucked that bad.

I applied in 2019, deferred for a year, and then joined in fall of 2021 when the pandemic was clearing up a little. In 2019, I was dissatisfied with my work. I recall thinking that I just wanted someone to tell me what to do and I’d do it. I tried that for a while, and liked that even less than the previous year of feeling overwhelmed by leading my first research projects. Personal reasons also played into me generally feeling down.

My year of deferring school ended up being wonderful. I didn’t feel pressure to perform at work because I knew I was starting a new program in the fall. Additionally, the remote-work, pandemic world enabled me to live for a month at a time in various cities across the US with friends, and I spent more time outdoors and hiking than I had ever before in my life. In hind sight, taking the pressure off was just what I needed. I did work I’m really proud of that year, and tee-ed up work for the coming years that I continue to be excited about.

I also wish someone had told me in 2018-2019 that getting started in research just takes time. That was my second year in industry, second year doing research full-time, and it honestly just takes time to build taste, meet collaborators you vibe with, and actually complete projects. The project that overwhelmed me in 2018 was extremely ambitious, and the last two years have been more-or-less motivated by actually doing that project well. Except this time, instead of being one of three people working on it for half a year, I’ve collaborated with dozens of people and written five papers so far.

Interdisciplinary work

If I were to say “language models memorize data” your immediate next few thoughts might include: “what does that mean for privacy / copyright” “is that a bad thing?” “what are the actual privacy risks of that?” I got attached to both the technical questions around understanding memorization and also the societal implications of that, and to answer those questions, I wanted to work with both HCI and legal scholars.

I think I could’ve done this at Google. It’s pretty common to collaborate with academia, and I could’ve worked to start a project in that area. Actually, I tried starting a collaboration like that for several years before starting the PhD, but because it was never my (or anyone elses’) primary project, they often didn’t get off the ground. Ironically, the current project I’m working on in this area got started with a Googler—sometimes it’s just a timing thing, and things will eventually shake out.

I don’t think it would’ve been as easy to work with legal or policy folks at Google. The lawyers that I have interacted with at Google have been tasked with reviewing content and actions that Googler’s take, and are less interested in writing up how the law might apply to a new use case. Additionally, doing that work at Google definitely biases it towards large tech companies.

One additional challenge to doing this work internally is that there are so many wonderful qualified people–how do you choose who to work with? How many people do you need to stay in sync with? 1

Collaboration

I’ve been fortunate to work with some peers who also like to pair-program. Let me tell you, that is the best feeling, and definitely when I’m most productive. I thought that the PhD would be full of potential peer collaborators that I could do that with and was so excited.

I also wanted to mentor others with supervision. In a PhD, this is often explicitly set up under the direction of your advisor.

In practice, it’s seems not hard to find younger students to mentor, though I haven’t done it yet. It is much harder to find people to collaborate with, seemingly because the PhD is an individual exercise where everyone writes a thesis at the end.

Curious about academia

After working for several years, I didn’t really remember what academia was like–and my view of what kind of a life I wanted to live had also changed quite dramatically.2 Part of my goal was to suss out whether I could find a good fit in academia.

But as you may have guessed, academia is just as political as everywhere else–possibly more so because there isn’t an agreed upon priority system. Everyone can make an argument for why their work is interesting and impactful, and some people you will agree more with, and others less so. In industry, there are a couple very visible metrics that can help with focusing and prioritization (i.e. product impact). Alignment towards a goal can help everyone evaluate and prioritize work.

People told me not to go

A lot of people told me not to go. When I expressed doubt about whether I should continue in a PhD, my advisor asked me if I was worried about disappointing people in my life if I were to tell them that I was leaving. Boy, most people in my life will be delighted to hear if I leave a PhD (in part because that means I can spend more time with them in SF). While many people are neutral: think it could be good for me to do but is all-together not necessary, many people ask me every time they see me: “wait why are you doing a PhD again?” This is in part why I’m writing this doc… There seems to be no correlation between whether or not the person I’m talking to has a PhD or not as to what they think I should do. Even people who loved their PhDs wiggle their eyebrows at me.

Obviously, I doubted my decision a lot because of this!

Future-proofing

At the same time that everyone was eye-brow wiggling at me, many people acknowledged that if you know you want to do research, having a PhD gives you that credibility. I think this is mostly for people or fields who are unfamiliar with your work. Today, there are many ML researchers without PhDs who have done amazing work.

I also got the advice that as a woman in ML, I could make my life easier by getting a PhD. Namely, why play life on hard mode?

Well to answer that question, getting a PhD is kind-of also hard mode! Just different kinds of hard.

Resources

The amount of money that seems to be a big deal in academia makes me sad. In particular because housing and food security have been in question for some students I’m aware of, and I cannot understand how anyone could allow someone they mentor to be in this position. The grad student stipends are not enough–even in a place like Ithaca– to have the mind-space to allow technical exploration. I cannot overstate how fortunate I feel to have worked for a few years before going to grad school.

Grad school after working

Several people I know who have come to grad school straight from undergrad have said that they wanted to do it now because they didn’t think that they would want to go back after they had tried industry. It’s definitely true that in some industries, “entry-level” assumes a PhD, and also true that not having a PhD can sometimes limit your career trajectory. It’s also true that leaving industry to go back to a PhD is difficult–it breaks a lot of momentum and sometimes asks you to leave friends and support networks. However, it can be done, and there are so many careers where having or not having a PhD could hardly make a difference that I think it’s worth exploring if you think you could be happy in one of those careers before doing a PhD. At the very least, save up some money so you actually have runway to take the freedom a PhD offers you and focus on that.

Art

I’ve been feeling that PhDs are very similar to artistic pursuits, and also very similar to running your own company. In all cases, you are often creating something new and tasked with resourcing your project–whether that’s looking for compute resources, funding, or collaborators. In all cases, you can either treat it as something “entirely” new, or create something in conversation with the context of the times.

Classes

In a world with infinite time, I too would love to spend hours learning about other parts of computer science. But this is not a world with infinite time, and I feel confined by required course-load.
