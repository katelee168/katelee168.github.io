Learning to fail

June 2017

How do we improve ourselves while continuing to love and be happy with where we are. Is there such a thing as being content? Can you learn and fail without anxiety?

I’ve felt continually pressured to succeed, some self-induced, some from the fear of silent disappointment. Some from the feeling of emptiness when there isn’t a string of accolades and compliments parading to your door, from deriving your own worth from what you’ve done and not who you are, from being praised for perfectionism, for being smart. Theoretical math has always proven a challenge for me. Theoretical math was a cube in the little game children play to learn shapes where they fit squares into square holes, and circles into circular holes, but it got stuffed into a circular hole and became distorted in my mind. I’ve never quite managed to recover all the pointy edges. Working on a proof, means failing all the time. It means, starting many different paths, finding dead ends, and starting again. But when you read a proof, you don’t see those many false starts, and you can’t imagine how anyone could have come up with this. You begin to doubt that you will ever figure this out.

It wasn’t until recently that I realized no one else got it the first time.

At the same time, it pained me to start, and stop, and start, and stop again, and I felt so imperfect, so stupid.

I wanted to devise a way for me to learn without feeling the pressure of perfection and the anxiety of failure. I wanted to learn something just for the sake of learning and not for any end product, any end proof. I wanted just the journey and none of the destination.

I planned to go to the studio three times a week for an hour each time, make pots, and destroy them.

I wanted to do my best on each pot, and treat them as if I were going to fire and glaze them. It was a lot harder to care about something when I knew I was going to destroy them later. So, I revised my initial plan to destroy the pots right after I made them, and instead, kept them on a shelf so that over the past three weeks, I’ve become rather attached to them. Destroying them, yesterday, was a lot harder than it might appear in the video.

The funny thing is that the project evolved and became its own destination. I found myself worrying whether I had made enough pots, whether they were big enough, whether they would break when they hit the cement. Anxiety had not gone, just transferred.

Though I only meant to spend nine hours in the studio, over the past three weeks I must have spent over twenty hours in the studio and gone almost every day the studio was open. I was so excited. On the last day, I was exhausted. My arms hurt from centering large mounds of clay, and my back hurt from hunching over. I’m grateful for this opportunity to take a break and reflect on what I’ve learned instead of continuing to power through. The pottery studio is closed for the summer, but I’m excited to return home and pick up pottery again at the studio near my house.

It feels easier to fail in pottery. Perhaps because those in the studio talk about their mistakes—the crack at the bottom of a plate, the ripple they made in a tall cylinder, or their own shot glasses. Perhaps because unlike working with my head, no one has ever told me that I was good working with my hands, and I didn’t have a reputation to live up to. Perhaps there’s just something about moving the earth with your fingers, time’s grasp removed from your bare arms, and the gentle hum of the wheel turning round and round.
