Privacy

Why do we care about privacy The US constitution recognizes privacy as a fundamental, human right. However, just what we mean by that has been a topic of debate throughout American history and especially in the last century. We may commonly understand privacy to enable individuals to live aspects of their lives outside of the public view. Privacy can sometimes be in contention with public interest, especially the privacy of public figures where actions they take or aspects of their lives may be important for the public to be aware of. While some techniques, like reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF), attempt to better “align” model generations to human preferences, the fundamental issue with respecting privacy remains understanding the context in which a phrase may or may not be sensitive. This issue is laid out in Helen Nissabaum’s 2009 book on contextual integrity (Nissenbaum 2009) which dissolves the notion of public or private data and instead asks that we consider the context in which the information in question is being shared. Different contexts call for different treatment of data. Techniques like RLHF currently fall short of addressing sensitivity because they do not make judgements for each data point and each generation as to whether the content is sensitive or not, and do not understand the context in which a generation is being requested. Any model that does not take into account both the context of the training data and the context in which a generation is created will necessarily fall short of the standards held by contextual integrity.

Privacy violations in generative models arise because the context in which data originated becomes divorced from the data through the process of collecting training data and training generative models.

Attribution and retrieval models differ from current generative models by connecting generations to sources. However, it is not enough to simply link the source. Linking the source to the generation can both improve and exacerbate privacy harms. Since these models still cannot judge whether the generations created, or the training data used, are sensitive or personal, knowing the sources of generations simply enables fact verification. If the source information was intended to be public, then, yes, knowing the source of the information is only helpful in the sense that we know the generation has validity. On the other hand, if the source information was not intended for a public audience, then knowing the source information furthers privacy harms by revealing, to the extent that the source is believable, that the generated information was indeed true. The extent to which retrieval and attribution models can improve privacy leaks, then, is limited to the extent that the data retrieved or trained on was intended to be shared publicly. However, we reiterate that attributions generated and training data retrieved may not actually be used for any given generation. Thus, whether attribution and retrieval models are able to improve accuracy assessments of generations is limited by the likelihood that those attributions and retrieved data were actually used to create the generation.

A common objection to the validity of privacy harms from generative models is that generations cannot be easily traced to an individual, and these potential harms are not cognizable. In “Privacy Harms,” (Citron and Solove 2022) argues that tests for cognizable privacy harms are incomplete as today’s norm of mass data collection violates the privacy of a large number of people an incognizable amount. Reactions to Peter’s work

What are reasonable technical safeguards that could be implemented? Reasonable standard of care? “Throughout this work we will not generally cover liability to the model creator for the model weights themselves. It is unresolved as to whether the model parameters themselves are infringing, and thus whether the model trainers themselves are liable” They make a distinction between model trainers and model deployers. So trainers would simply be the people creating the tool to potentially infringe. Which, under other circumstances did have liability “When the model is capable of outputting both transformative and non-transformative content, it is also unresolved how the model itself (and model creators) should be treated as a function of secondary liability. The extraction of non-transformative content, according to our experiments and others, is often not straightforward. It requires effort on the part of model users and deployers to identify an extraction mechanism.” This is not quite right. Verbatim content can be easy to identify. Near-verbatim content is also easy to identify if you know what “non-verbatim” means. Whether the content is transformative or not depends on the context and content of the generation. Thus, whether the generation is verbatim or near-verbatim, or not at all is a factor that goes into judging whether that generation is transformative or not Similarly, attribution is another factor. Additionally, adversarially acting to extract non-transformative content is often not straightforward. However, intent to extract non-transformative content is not required for model users to create non-transformative content. “But it is not obvious that the DMCA safe harbors apply to generated content. For example, in 2019, Amazon lost on a motion to dismiss when its algorithms selected copyrighted material to host on the website (Williams-Sonoma, Inc. v. Amazon.com, Inc., N.D. Cal. 2021, Order on Motion to Dismiss). The court was unconvinced that Amazon was eligible for safe harbor under the DMCA. They stated that to establish safe harbor the content must be stored “at the direction of the user” (Mavrix Photographs, LLC v. Livejournal Inc., 9th Cir. 2017, at 1052). This may mean that generated content does not have the same safe harbor and that post-hoc take-downs are not sufficient to reduce liability.26 As such, filtering of generated content before a takedown request is ever received may be more important while the courts determine the applicability of DMCA to generated content.” And possibly also related to sec 230 “For example, if a dataset house thousands of distinct images of a given NBA player with a distinctive tattoo, it may be difficult to deduplicate all of these images in a way that prevents the model from learning to reproduce the tattoo verbatim. Thus, situations like Alexander v. Take-Two Interactive Software, Inc. (S.D. Ill. 2020) might still occur with this strategy.” This is concept vs. verbatim memorization

Discussion

The interesting thing about thinking about these models is that you can’t just ignore the technical decision that were made, however, at the same time, you can’t treat them as a black box either. So the rules and regulations we come up with with regards to generative models need to adapt to slightly different implementations of the models. And there will always be changes that reduce the longevity of those implementations. ## Archive
